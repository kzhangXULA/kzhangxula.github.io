The DATA MINING AND BIOINFORMATICS GROUP at the Physics and Computer Science Department, Xavier University of Louisiana is led by Dr. Kun (Karen) Zhang. Our primary research falls into many aspects of machine learning/data mining, big data analystics and bioinformatics. Machine learning/data mining is one of the most exciting areas in computer science. It is the study and development of novel algorithms to discover interesting regularities from large amount of data. Bioinformatics focuses on applying computational and statistical methods to solving practical problems in the biological and biomedical domains. We are particularly interested in developing simple, unconventional, but effective models that can automatically learn from complex and diverse data sources. Such models are in high demand since scientific advances have led to an overwhelming volume of data of ever-increasing size and complexity while the development of automated methods to extract knowledge from these data is still in its infancy.

Kun (Karen) Zhang is a Professor of Computer Science and the Faculty Lead of Bioinformatics Core of Xavier RCMI Center for Cancer Research at Xavier University of Louisiana. She earned a PhD in Computer Science with a concentration on Machine Learning and Data Mining from Tulane University. Dr. Zhang is an established big data scientist, and has extensive experience in cancer bioinformatics and methodology development for multimodal multi-omics data integration and analyses. She is the recipient of "The Best Application Paper Award" at the 6th IEEE International Conference on Data Mining, the championship of the 8th IEEE ICDM2008 Data Mining Contest, and "the Top-10 Data Mining Case Studies" selected by the IEEE 10th International Conference on Data Mining. As the PI and co-PI on multiple NIH, DOD and NSF grants, she has been leading the effort in developing approaches and tools to identify promising risk molecular signatures (i.e. genes/pathways/network modules) with clinical utilities for different cancer types. Her most recent study aims to develop novel bioinformatics and statistical machine learning (e.g. deep learning) algorithms for exploring biological mechanisms underlying cancer health disparities using systems biology approaches and multiple information sources, including population-based cancer registries data from the SEER program and individual-level multi-omics data (i.e. NGS, gene expression and SNPs) collected by the NCBI dbGaP, GEO, etc. Some findings resulting from this project have been published by Bioinformatics, Cancer Epidemiology, Biomarkers & Prevention, Frontiers in Oncology and Scientific Reports, among her 80 peer-reviewed articles in computational biology and data sciences.
